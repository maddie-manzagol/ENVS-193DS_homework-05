Introduction

Sarracenia, commonly known as pitcher plants, are carnivorous wetland flora native to North America that thrive in sunny, wet, nutrient-poor habitats (Horner). Sarracenia may be of interest to people because their predation strategies can have indirect as well as direct impacts on the population dynamics and community structure of freshwater plankton and bacterial communities due to their interactions at higher trophic levels which control bacterial densities and community structure (Cochran-Stafira). Sarracenia may also be of interest to people because their carnivorous behavior causes them to not follow the ‘world-wide leaf economics spectrum’ which is defined as an allometric relationship between nutrients, maximal net photosynthetic rates, and physiology (notably leaf size) (Farnsworth and Ellison). The reason their leaf traits diverge from the leaf economics spectrum is due to the fact that carnivorous plants derive most of their nutrients from capturing and digesting prey, implying low photosynthetic rates and low photosynthetic nitrogen and phosphorous use efficiencies (Farnsworth and Ellison). Since prey capture is their main source of nutrient uptake, prey capture is known to be indicative of their fitness in the sense that increased prey capture aids in survival, growth, and reproduction (Horner). Due to the impact on the individual fitness of Sarracenia plants, it is important to understand how Sarracenia traits aid in prey capture as well as how Sarracenia react to changes in the abundance of prey (Horner). Additionally, it is valuable to predict individual biomass of Sarracenia based on morphological, physiological, or taxonomic characteristics because the prediction models offer insight into their growth patterns, resource allocation, and ecological roles, potentially shedding light on larger ecological processes, as well as, how humans can adopt effective management practices. This study investigates the effects of prey availability on photosynthetic rate (Amass), chlorophyll fluorescence, growth, architecture, and foliar nutrient and chlorophyll content of ten pitcher plant (Sarracenia) species. The study questions how prey availability impacts Sarracenia growth and health, as well as, if morphological and physiological allometric relationships of Sarracenia resemble allometric relationships of non-carnivorous plants when nutrients are not a limiting resource. These questions formulated the hypothesis that increased prey availability would stimulate Amass, increase photosynthetic N- and P-use efficiencies, increase relative biomass allocation to photosynthetically efficient, non-predatory phyllodes rather than pitchers, and thereby increase overall plant biomass and reduce stress-related chlorophyll fluorescence.

Methods

  1. Sampling Methods

120 Sarracenia plants ranging from small, intermediate, and large species size were used for the study were collected from the sampling site: Harvard Forest Greenhouse (Ellison and Farnsworth). To conduct the study, plants were fed once a week for 7 weeks straight (Ellison and Farnsworth). Two plants of each Sarracenia species were assigned to one of six feeding levels in a regression design ranging from 0 - 0.25g of finely ground wasps per feeding (for small species), 0 - 0.5g (for species of intermediate sizes), and 0 - 1.0g (for large species) (Ellison and Farnsworth). “Aboveground size” and “Amass” were measured prior to commencing treatments, meanwhile all other parameters were measured during the duration of the study (Ellison and Farnsworth).

f. Creating the null and full model with accompanying discussion

To determine how species and physiological characteristics perditc biomass, we fit multiple linear models. The null model creates a regression model that assumes no realtionship between totmass and the predictor variables. On the other hand, the full model creates a regression model that assumes a relationship between totmass and the predictor variables in order to predict biomass.

Code 
#create linear regression models for null and full model
null <- lm(totmass ~ 1, data= plant_subset)
full <- lm(totmass ~ species + feedlevel + sla + chlorophyll + amass + num_lvs + num_phylls, data= plant_subset)
  1. Visual and statistical assumption checks for the full model with accompanying discussion

We visually assesed normaility and homoskedascitity using diagnostic plots for the full model and found that the data is appears normal, but not homoskedastic because the data is not scattered entirely random. We also tested for normality using the Shaprio-Wilk test and found non-normality of residuals. We then retested for homoscedasticity using the Breusch-Pagan test and detected non-constant error variance meaning the data is heteroscedastic.

  1. Model construction with visual and statistical assumption checks for three additional models with justification

To address the question, what set of predictor variables best explains the response, we applied three more linear models using the predictor variables species, feedlevel, and num_lvs. The species variable was chosen as a predictor variable because it is probable that different species have different biomasses. The model comparing species as predictor variable and totmass as the response variable was found to be normal and homoscedastic. The feedlevel variable was chosen as a predictor variable because different feed levels are associated with different nutrient level. Different nutrient levels are indicative of growth and overall health which contributes to individual’s biomass. The num_lvs variable was chosen as a predictor variable because the more leaves an individual has, the more biomass an individual has. The models comparing feedlevel and num_lvs to predict totmass, respectively, both were found to be non-normal, yet homoscedastic.

Results

We found that the full_log model including species, feedlevel, sla, chlorophyll, amass, num_lvs, and num_phylls predictors best predicted totmass because the full_log model yields the lowest AIC value indicating that this model has the best predictive capacity while remaining as simple as possible. Overall, the full_log model was statistically significant (R^2= 0.8687, F(15, 87)= 38.38, p<0.001). With all else held constant, the full_log model indicates a significant relationship between totmass and num_lvs (p< 0.001). Comparing to the alabamensis species of pitcher plants, the leucophylla species are likely to have a biomass that is 1.7 higher (95% confidence level, [1.25, 2.16]) and the psittacina species are likely to have a biomass that is 1.6 lower (95% confidence level, [-2.05,-1.23]). However it was found that some of the predictors such as, the jonesii and purpurea species, amass, and num_phylls, did not significantly predict totmass (p= 0.107, p=0.155, p= 0.436, p= 0.446, respectively). In conclusion, our model highlights that variables such as number of leaves, feed level, specific leaf area, chlorophyll, and specific species type (lata, flava, leucophylla, minor, psittacina, rosea, and rubra) are all significant variables in predicting the biomass of one individual pitch plant. Focusing on the number of leaves, individuals producing more pitchers and phyllodes will predictably accumulate more biomass than individuals who produce less pitches and phyllodes.
